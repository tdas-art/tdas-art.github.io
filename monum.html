Civic innovation · Comms strategy

MONUM, City of Boston

City governments have a lot to say. Getting residents to actually care is the hard part. That was my job.

MONUM — the Mayor's Office of New Urban Mechanics — is Boston's civic innovation team. They design and test new approaches to how the city serves residents, working across departments and with communities to prototype, pilot, and scale solutions that make public systems more accessible, effective, and human-centered. I joined as a Communications and Design Intern.

The question I kept asking

Government has a lot to say, and most of it never actually lands. My job wasn't to make MONUM louder. It was to figure out who a piece of content was actually for, and whether it was reaching them — the same question that shows up in every project I take on.

The idea that was mine

Nobody assigned this one. I noticed Halloween as a moment MONUM could use to show up differently online — something warmer and more human than a typical government account. I pitched it to the team, scouted the locations myself, and shot it.

The craft that got noticed

For MONUM's food waste recovery initiative, the participants and interview format were already set before I joined the project — but the story that made it to air was shaped entirely in the edit. I shot and edited both a short-form and long-form version, going through roughly 45 iterations before it felt right. It's now live on the City of Boston's official YouTube channel and featured on boston.gov's Haymarket Food Recovery page.

Add: embed of the boston.gov Haymarket Food Recovery video

Two different kinds of work — one where the idea was mine from the start, one where the craft made someone else's real work land. Both taught me the same thing: good public communication is never really about the platform. It's about whether the person on the other end feels like the message was actually meant for them.
